1. Executive Summary

This memo reviews 23 recent papers on Generative Engine Optimization (GEO), AI search visibility, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), citation behavior, structured website content, platform auditing, and financial-document RAG. The main shift in this literature is that website visibility is no longer determined only by whether a page ranks highly in a traditional search engine results page. In AI-mediated search, a page must be retrieved, selected, cited, summarized faithfully, and sometimes used as evidence for a generated recommendation.

The most central papers for the memo are GEO: Generative Engine Optimization, SAGEO Arena, What Generative Search Engines Like, E-GEO, How Generative AI Disrupts Search, Measuring Google AI Overviews, FinRAGBench-V, and Enabling Large Language Models to Generate Text with Citations. Together, they provide the conceptual foundation, optimization mechanisms, platform-level measurement evidence, e-commerce relevance, and finance-specific RAG/citation framework.

For a finance/economics project, the literature points to a clear opportunity: measure AI visibility as an economic outcome. Candidate dependent variables include citation rate, source overlap with traditional search, answer inclusion, recommendation probability, product rank change, citation position, citation absorption, claim fidelity, unsupported-claim rate, and visual citation precision/recall for financial reports. These measures could be linked to firm characteristics, website structure, disclosure quality, publisher business models, product-market competition, and potential downstream outcomes such as traffic, revenue, conversion, or market concentration.

3. Key Findings from the CS Literature

3.1 GEO reframes SEO as an optimization problem for generated answers

GEO: Generative Engine Optimization is the foundational paper because it formalizes the creator-side problem: how can content owners modify text so that generative engines cite or include their content more prominently? The key contribution is not only the term GEO, but also the measurement idea that visibility can be quantified by position-adjusted word share and subjective impression. This is directly useful for finance/econ work because firms, publishers, and product sellers may care less about blue-link ranking and more about whether AI systems quote, cite, summarize, or recommend them.

Later GEO papers deepen this idea. What Generative Search Engines Like provides a stronger recent empirical anchor by identifying content preferences and building AutoGEO-style optimization methods. SAGEO Arena adds a crucial pipeline insight: visibility may differ across retrieval, reranking, and generation. A website could be retrieved but not cited, or cited but not influential in the final answer. This matters for empirical design because measuring only final citations can miss where the visibility loss occurs.

3.2 Website adaptation is likely to involve structure, evidence density, and stage-aware content

The GEO literature suggests that optimization is not merely keyword stuffing. Several papers point to evidence-rich and structured content as a more plausible adaptation path. Feature-level optimization papers show that controllable page features can affect citation visibility, while structural-feature papers emphasize headings, paragraph chunking, lists, tables, links, and schema-like organization. Agentic and ecosystem-level GEO papers further suggest that firms may not only rewrite a single page, but coordinate product pages, FAQs, reviews, comparison pages, support pages, and third-party evidence to increase the probability that AI search agents encounter and trust a target source.

The e-commerce paper E-GEO is especially relevant for economic analysis because its outcome is product ranking rather than only citation share. This makes it easier to connect GEO to seller competition, platform-mediated discovery, product differentiation, and consumer choice. In a finance/econ setting, the analogous question is whether AI systems change which firms, products, or publishers become visible when consumers ask purchase, investment, insurance, credit, or financial-information queries.

3.3 Live AI search systems redistribute visibility relative to traditional search

The platform-measurement papers are important because they move beyond controlled GEO benchmarks. How Generative AI Disrupts Search compares Google Search, Gemini, and AI Overviews and finds low overlap among sources across traditional and generative interfaces. This supports the claim that AI search can reallocate attention away from the sources that dominate traditional search results. Measuring Google AI Overviews strengthens the economic relevance by measuring activation, source quality, claim fidelity, and publisher impact. These papers help motivate an econ project on whether AI search changes traffic allocation, publisher bargaining power, ad exposure, and source concentration.

Search Arena and news-citation papers add two further insights. First, citations and longer, search-supported responses can correlate with user preference, which means citation presentation itself may shape perceived credibility. Second, AI search can behave like an algorithmic gatekeeper by concentrating citations among particular sources. These mechanisms are directly relevant to market concentration, media economics, and platform power.

3.4 Citation quality and financial-document RAG are essential for finance applications

For finance/econ work, being cited is not enough. The cited source must actually support the claim. Enabling Large Language Models to Generate Text with Citations is therefore a key anchor because it distinguishes answer correctness from citation precision and recall. This distinction matters in finance because an AI system may cite an annual report, 10-K, earnings slide, or prospectus without using the correct table, chart, or textual evidence.

The finance-specific RAG papers make this point more concrete. FinRAGBench-V evaluates multimodal financial RAG with visual citation, while REAL-MM-RAG and LaRA show the relevance of financial statements, tables, slides, and long-context documents. These papers suggest that finance/econ projects should not only ask whether a firm appears in an AI answer, but also whether the AI system retrieves the correct financial evidence, cites the correct page/block, and avoids outdated or unsupported claims. HOH and DailyQA add that temporal freshness is a major concern, especially when company facts, executives, market statistics, regulations, and macroeconomic indicators change over time.

4. Measures and Empirical Outcomes Useful for Finance/Econ Work

The literature provides a strong menu of measurable outcomes that could be translated into finance/econ variables. These can be grouped into visibility, citation, quality, and economic-adaptation measures.

Outcome family Possible variables Finance/econ interpretation
Visibility Citation rate, answer inclusion, source rank, rank change, retrieval hit rate, recommendation probability Whether a firm, seller, publisher, or product becomes visible in AI-mediated search.
Citation influence Citation position, position-adjusted word count, impression score, citation absorption, source overlap How much the cited source actually shapes the final AI answer, not just whether it is listed.
Evidence quality Citation precision/recall, claim fidelity, unsupported-claim rate, contradiction rate, visual citation accuracy Whether AI-generated financial or consumer information is verifiable and source-supported.
Platform comparison Jaccard overlap, rank-biased overlap, AIO activation rate, cross-system visibility differences Whether ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Google AI Overviews, or traditional Google reward different sources.
Strategic adaptation Changes in page structure, schema markup, evidence density, FAQ design, product-description rewriting, investor-relations formatting Whether firms adapt content for AI systems rather than only human readers or traditional SEO.

5. Open Gaps and Recommended Next Steps

The main gap is that the CS literature is strong on benchmarks, optimization methods, and platform audits, but still limited on downstream economic outcomes. Most papers measure visibility, citation, rank, retrieval, or answer quality. They usually do not observe actual clicks, sales, conversions, advertising revenue, publisher traffic, investor behavior, or firm financial outcomes. This gap is exactly where a finance/econ project can contribute.

A promising research design would construct a firm- or product-level panel of AI visibility across multiple systems. The project could query ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Google AI Overviews using standardized consumer, product, or financial-information prompts. It could then compare AI visibility with traditional Google ranking, website structure, schema markup, firm size, domain authority, disclosure quality, product category, and downstream economic proxies. If repeated over time, the design could also study whether firms change website structure after AI search systems become more prominent.

  • Firm strategy: Do firms rewrite product pages, investor-relations pages, FAQs, or structured data to become more visible to AI search systems?
  • Market concentration: Do AI search systems concentrate citations among already-dominant sources, or do they create visibility opportunities for smaller firms and publishers?
  • Platform power: How much do AI search systems diverge from traditional search, and which platforms redistribute source visibility the most?
  • Information quality: Are AI-cited financial claims faithful to underlying documents, especially tables, charts, earnings slides, and reports?
  • Temporal validity: How often do AI systems rely on outdated company, product, regulatory, or market information?
